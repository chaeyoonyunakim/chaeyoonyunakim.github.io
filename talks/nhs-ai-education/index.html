03

What we learned

Governance framing, domain expertise, and the barrier to formal thinking

The EU AI Act as the motivating framework

Five obligations, taught as engineering requirements:

1Accountability
2Transparency
3Fairness
4Human oversight
5Auditability
Learners could name which specification property — or which EU AI Act obligation — a given AI output violated.

Governance framing lowers the barrier to formal thinking

  • Framing correctness requirements as governance artefacts substantially reduced the perceived barrier — regardless of academic stage or professional seniority
  • Pre/post-conditions and invariants became legible when expressed as technical evidence for documented acceptance criteria and regulatory obligations
  • Learners grew more confident evaluating AI-generated code critically
  • [Add a learner quote or concrete before/after example]

Domain expertise is a pedagogical resource

  • Existing NHS knowledge — clinical, operational, or analytical — accelerated engagement with property-based tests and reproducibility manifests
  • Learners could readily articulate what a pipeline failure would mean in practice
  • Those who understand what is at stake are strongly motivated to acquire the formal vocabulary to prevent it

The reframing

Formal methods shift from arcane overhead to necessary practice.

Correctness reasoning is not separate from AI ethics

Not just: does your code work?
But: can you prove it, audit it, and defend it — to a clinician, a policymaker, or a regulator?
  • A replicable model for programming education in the age of responsible AI
  • Extends beyond the NHS: other public-sector areas and high-confidentiality private-sector domains
